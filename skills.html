Gardening

Buying my first home was the catalyst for my interest in gardening. My interest in improving the appearance and atmosphere of my yard grew into an interest in native plants. From there I added a vegetable garden. Eventually I removed every bit of grass on my property and turned my yard into a native plant and kitchen garden. A desire to improve my growing skills lead to volunteering on a local certified organic farm...which lead to an internship...which eventually lead to quiting my job for a full-time season-long on-farm internship on another farm. That lead to a full-time season-long on-farm apprenticeship the next year.

During these years I also supported other growers by managing awinter farmers' market. I've worked with the herd at an organic dairy. I've farmed with tractors and horses. I helped create and was the first program coordinator for my local food banks' Garden and Gleaning Programs. I've shared my skills and knowledge with other gardeners as the manager of three community gardens.

These days I have a rather elaborate ornamental garden and food forest at my current home. My current home is in a warmer hardiness zone and as a result I've developed a passion for growing dahlias. This fall was my first dividing and storing tubers. I look forward to growing up my stock and perhaps I'll start to dabble in breeding new varieties.

All this is to say that by following my curiosity I went from being a homeowner with a yard with which she didn't know what to do to an avid hobby gardener to eventually developing my skills enough to becoming a professional grower and gardening educator.

Here I am driving a team of Belgian draft horses, Indy and Lager, while harvesting potatoes.

My first attempt at dividing and storing dahlia tubers this fall. Those eyes are so tiny and so hard to see! I'm so grateful to all the wonderful growers who shared their knowledge online, but if I could point you towards just one, it would be Swan Island Dahlias here in the Willamette Valley.

This dahlia that I grew was supposed to be Jennifer's Wedding but I don't believe that variety is supposed to have yellow disc floret buds (the center part). I don't know if the tuber I purchased was mislabeled or if I got a sport (variant) but either way I love her.

Having a kitchen garden is still quite special to me. Both for the satisfaction of growing my own and for the delicious taste of fresh picked produce. These tomatoes and peppers were all grown by me in the backyard. If you have a eye for peppers you might notice the ghost peppers in this photo. They were hot hot hot!

Baking

Long before I started gardening I learned to bake at the knee of my Grandma Mary and my mom. I'm not sure which I love more: eating my homebaked treats or sharing them with others. One of my fondest memories, which speaks to the joy spread by sharing baked goods, is from the year I lived in Israel and I received a box of homemade chocolate chip cookies from my mom. Those cookies had traveled internationally in not so timely a manner, but the homebaked deliciousnes help up.

I enjoy baking for others so much that a few years ago I briefly flirted with the idea of opening my own home-based bakery, which I would have named The Piefectionist, under Oregonâ€™s Home Baking Bill. I even played around with making my nacent business a website.

While I've moved on from the idea of starting a bakery and am excited about my current journey learning programming, I feel the way I described myself for that venture still holds true...

I believe in love and community and I believe that joy comes in all forms...including the form of baked goods. The guiding philosoply (or the Baking Philosophy if you will) of The Piefectionist is "Spreading Joy Through Baked Goods." I grew up baking with my Jewish grandma in Cleveland, Ohio. I learned to love pie as an adult in South Central Indiana. Nature and nurture collaborated to make me a perfectionist. It all comes together in my love of creating beautiful pies and pastries.

While I may have started baking things like chocolate chip cookies and chocolate cake as a kid, as an adult my repetoire has expanded. Over the years, first with the help of cookbooks from the library, and later with the help of so many wonderful online blogs and resources, I have followed my curiousity and learned how to bake more and more complex items. Some of my favorites to make are sourdough anything, pies, piecups (tarts), rugalach (apricot!!!), huckleberry or blueberry buckles, and babka (I don't want to start too lively of a debate, but I'm a cinnamon babka fan myself...but I've made plently of chocolate ones for others).

While I make both free form loaves and loaves in pans, I'm highlighting these whole wheat sandwich loaves as they involved a technique new-to-me that involved pre-cooking some of the flour to pre-gelatinize it. It gave the crumb a wonderful texture. This recipe came from Marizo, the blogger and baker of The Perfect Loaf. He is my most trusted bread resource.

I have tried a lot of different sourdough bagel recipes. I'm pretty sure these pictured here are also from a recipe from Marizo. Even if this batch wasn't I can wholeheartedly recommend his recipe. One of the things I love about baking, especially with sourdough, is learning to understand all the factors that impact your final product. Time, temperature, moisture,ingredients, and patience are some of the biggest factors I've learned about. Those all played together to create these larger wholed bagels. Other batches I've made have come out looking quite different.

If you've ever nurtured a sourdough starter you know that you end up with tons of discard. I'm loathe to throw away food, so I like to find ways to use it up. Making sourdough discard pasta dough is one way I love to use it up. Here's a picture of some homemade ravioli I made this fall.

Quilting

I'm always curious about something new and a few years ago I gave myself permission to take the time for another deep dive into a new skill. I wanted to get good at sewing. Not only does sewing speak to the part of me that likes to learn how things are made, but my maternal grandfather was a Paris-trained furrier. As a kid when I wasn't baking with my grandma I was often sewing with my grandpa. He made me and my sibling and cousins rabbit-fur teddy bears, nightgowns, and other special homemade gifts. I still have (but don't currently sew on) his 1920s Singer Sewing machine. It was originally a treadle machine that he converted to electric with a kit from Singer. While I did some simple sewing as a kid I hadn't used a sewing machine in years until the summer of 2018.

Initially my interest was in learning to make my own clothes but I thought I should start with something a little simpler so I started with quilting. I fell in love with quilting and haven't gotten around to learning to sew clothing yet. I trust my curiousity will lead me there eventually!

Teaching myself to quilt from library books and youtube was actually what gave me the confidence and trust in myself to learn to program. In learning to quilt I built up my ability to be diligent about self-directed learning. It also taught me to learn new things and problem solve on the fly. Little did I know how welll that would translate to programming!

Here's a glimpse of my largest, an only WIP (working in progress). It's a triangle quilt for a standard full bed. The top is pieced but the back is not yet assembled and thus nor is it quilted yet. Committing to learning programming meant putting my sewing machine away for the foreseeable future and packing up and storing this WIP. But it'll be all the more satisfying when I again have the time to sew and finish my larest project to date.
